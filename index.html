Math Concepts

Section 1: Notation and Symbols

Mathematical notation and symbols are a universal language used to represent numbers, operations, relationships, and expressions concisely. Symbols such as +, −, ×, /, and = are fundamental in expressing equations, such as a + b = c. Grouping symbols like (), [], and {} prioritize operations, e.g., (a + b) × c.

Section 2: Divisibility Rules

Divisibility rules are shortcuts to determine if a number is divisible by another:

  • Divisible by 2: Last digit is even (e.g., 4, 8).
  • Divisible by 3: Sum of digits divisible by 3 (e.g., 123 → 1+2+3=6).
  • Divisible by 5: Last digit is 0 or 5 (e.g., 25, 30).
Section 3: Rules for Exponents

Exponents represent repeated multiplication. Key rules:

  • Product Rule: a² × a³ = a⁵
  • Quotient Rule: a⁵ ÷ a² = a³
  • Zero Exponent: a⁰ = 1
Section 4: Special Factoring Techniques

Factoring simplifies expressions. Special techniques include:

  • Difference of Squares: x² − a² = (x + a)(x − a).
  • Perfect Square Trinomial: x² + 2ax + a² = (x + a)².
  • Sum of Cubes: x³ + a³ = (x + a)(x² − ax + a²).
Section 5: Factoring Polynomials

Polynomials can be factored by extracting common factors, grouping terms, or using special patterns like difference of squares. For example:

  • Common Factor: 2x² + 6x = 2x(x + 3).
  • Grouping: x² + 3x + 2 = (x + 1)(x + 2).
Section 6: Summary of Formulas and Properties of Lines

Key formulas for lines include:

  • Slope-Intercept Form: y = mx + b, where m is slope and b is the y-intercept.
  • Point-Slope Form: y − y₁ = m(x − x₁), useful for graphs.

Simple Interest: I = P × r × t, where P is principal, r is annual rate, and t is time in years.

Section 12: Metric and U.S. Customary Units

Conversions: 1 inch = 2.54 cm, 1 pound = 0.454 kg, 1 gallon = 3.785 liters.

Inflation: A = P(1 + r)^t, where P is initial value, r is rate, and t is time in years.

Depreciation: V = P(1 − r)^t.

Algebra 1

Algebra 1 Formulas

Section: Slope of a Line

The slope of a line represents its steepness and is calculated as:

Formula: m = (y₂ - y₁) / (x₂ - x₁)

Where (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂) are two points on the line. For example, if the points are (1, 2) and (3, 6):

m = (6 - 2) / (3 - 1) = 4 / 2 = 2

Section: Slope-Intercept Form

The slope-intercept form of a linear equation is written as:

Formula: y = mx + b

Where:

  • m is the slope of the line.
  • b is the y-intercept (the point where the line crosses the y-axis).

Example: If m = 2 and b = 3, then the equation is y = 2x + 3.

Section: Point-Slope Form

The point-slope form of a linear equation is used when the slope m and a point on the line (x₁, y₁) are known:

Formula: y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)

Example: If m = 3 and the point is (2, 4), the equation is:

y - 4 = 3(x - 2) → y = 3x - 6 + 4 → y = 3x - 2

Section: Standard Form

The standard form of a linear equation is written as:

Formula: Ax + By = C

Where:

  • A, B, and C are integers.
  • A and B cannot both be 0.

Example: The equation 3x + 2y = 12 is in standard form.

Section: Quadratic Formula

The quadratic formula is used to find solutions (roots) of a quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0:

Formula: x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / 2a

Example: For the equation 2x² + 4x - 6 = 0, substitute a = 2, b = 4, and c = -6:

x = [ -4 ± √(4² - 4(2)(-6)) ] / 2(2) → x = [-4 ± √(16 + 48)] / 4 → x = [-4 ± √64] / 4 → x = (-4 ± 8) / 4

Solutions: x = 1 and x = -3.

Section: Factoring Quadratics

Factoring is used to rewrite a quadratic equation in the form (x + p)(x + q) = 0, making it easier to find the roots:

Example: Factor x² + 5x + 6:

(x + 2)(x + 3) = 0

Roots: x = -2 and x = -3.

Section: Distance Formula

The distance formula calculates the distance between two points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂) in a plane:

Formula: d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]

Example: For points (1, 2) and (4, 6):

d = √[(4 - 1)² + (6 - 2)²] → d = √[3² + 4²] → d = √(9 + 16) → d = √25 = 5

Section: Midpoint Formula

The midpoint formula finds the point exactly halfway between two points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

Formula: M = [(x₁ + x₂) / 2, (y₁ + y₂) / 2]

Example: For points (2, 3) and (4, 7):

M = [(2 + 4) / 2, (3 + 7) / 2] → M = [6 / 2, 10 / 2] → M = (3, 5)

Algebra 2 Formulas

Section: Compound Interest

Compound interest is calculated based on the principal amount and the accumulated interest from previous periods. The formulas are:

  • Annual Compounding: A = P(1 + r)^t
  • Periodic Compounding: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)
  • Continuous Compounding: A = Pe^(rt)

Where:

  • A: Final amount
  • P: Principal (initial amount)
  • r: Annual interest rate (decimal)
  • t: Time (years)
  • n: Number of compounding periods per year
  • e: Euler's number (~2.718)

Example: If P = $1,000, r = 0.05, t = 3, and n = 4 (quarterly), the amount is:

A = 1000(1 + 0.05/4)^(4×3) = 1000(1.0125)^12 ≈ $1157.63

Section: Logarithmic Properties

Logarithms are the inverses of exponents. Key properties include:

  • Product Rule: logₐ(xy) = logₐ(x) + logₐ(y)
  • Quotient Rule: logₐ(x/y) = logₐ(x) − logₐ(y)
  • Power Rule: logₐ(xⁿ) = n × logₐ(x)
  • Change of Base Formula: logₐ(x) = log(x)/log(a)

Example: Simplify log₂(32):

log₂(32) = log₂(2⁵) = 5 × log₂(2) = 5 × 1 = 5

Section: Exponential Growth and Decay

Exponential growth and decay are modeled using:

  • Growth: y = y₀e^(kt)
  • Decay: y = y₀e^(-kt)

Where:

  • y₀: Initial amount
  • k: Growth or decay rate
  • t: Time
  • e: Euler's number (~2.718)

Example: If a population of 100 grows at a rate of 5% annually, its size after 3 years is:

y = 100e^(0.05×3) ≈ 116.18

Section: Matrices and Determinants

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers. The determinant of a 2x2 matrix is calculated as:

Matrix: [[a, b], [c, d]]

Determinant: det = ad − bc

Example: For the matrix [[2, 3], [4, 5]]:

det = (2)(5) − (3)(4) = 10 − 12 = −2

Section: Polynomial Division

Polynomial long division is used to divide one polynomial by another. For example:

Divide x³ + 2x² − x − 2 by x − 1:

Solution:

Step 1: Divide the first term of the dividend by the first term of the divisor: x³ ÷ x = x².

Step 2: Multiply the entire divisor by x²: x²(x − 1) = x³ − x².

Step 3: Subtract: (x³ + 2x²) − (x³ − x²) = 3x².

Step 4: Repeat for all terms. The result is:

Quotient: x² + 3x + 2, Remainder: 0.
